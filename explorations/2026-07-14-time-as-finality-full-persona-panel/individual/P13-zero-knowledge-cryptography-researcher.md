## Steelman thesis

Possibility to Capability is really an attempt to build a sound claim-transport protocol: sources commit to a statement, evidence bundle, construction, and dependency graph, while a receiver checks witness relations without rewriting source truth or composing partial successes. Its distinctive contribution is an epistemic protocol that preserves refusal, null, fork, and revision when no typed claim is witnessed.

## Summary of understanding

The six terms are statement types, not stages: admissible alternatives, fixed-family transformation, stable record, operational access, normalized task-set change, and a scoped candidate for non-factorable settlement. Cryptographically, Frozen-Packet resembles commitment to a public instance; gates resemble witness verifiers; the one-chain rule resembles consistency in composition; and source/receiver separation distinguishes statement generation from verification. This is analogy, not an implemented zero-knowledge system.

The evidence establishes deterministic behavior on synthetic fixtures and a disciplined provenance stop. It establishes no accepted real packet, completed physical gate run, capability change, or finality result. GU-001 remains `NOT_YET_IMPORTABLE`, so its visible artifacts cannot be aggregated into evidence for either candidate or rival.

## Strongest insight

The deepest insight is that explanatory claims should be witness-carrying and branch-consistent. More important than binding bytes is binding every conclusion to one construction, dependency structure, and argument chain. Preserving `BLOCKED`, `INDETERMINATE`, forks, and `HIERARCHY_REVISION` prevents absence of a valid witness from being laundered into acceptance.

## Strongest criticism

Protocol soundness is not physical soundness. A verifier shows only that a witness satisfies its encoded relation, not that the relation captures nature, evidence is sufficient, or normalization is non-question-begging. “No factor found” is bounded search failure, not proof that none exists. Without a formal, independently justified search universe or checkable impossibility certificate, finality risks becoming a prestigious residual category.

## Hidden assumptions

The approach assumes canonical serialization and identity; trustworthy authorship, not merely matching digests; suitable hash security; decidable witness relations; enumerable admissible constructions; and composition rules making local witnesses jointly consistent. It also assumes task vocabularies, resource frames, access, and control can be fixed without encoding the capability verdict, and that source meaning survives formalization.

## Rose

The strongest current feature is failure-preserving source sovereignty. The GU preflight demonstrates that the receiver will stop at missing provenance rather than fabricate a packet, infer convergence from visible artifacts, or let downstream gates masquerade as run. That is unusually good protocol hygiene.

## Bud

The most promising development is a proof-carrying assessment format: define each gate as a typed relation over a committed packet and require machine-checkable certificates for positive and negative claims where possible. Selective-disclosure or zero-knowledge techniques could later let a source prove limited predicates about sensitive evidence without disclosing the witness, but only if the receiver contract explicitly admits such proofs and does not mistake privacy for truth.

## Thorn

The principal danger is cryptographic theater: reproducible hashes, manifests, and passing validators may create confidence that exceeds the semantics. A perfectly bound packet can faithfully commit to inadequate evidence, a circular relation, or a biased construction. Interface completion must never be counted as scientific confirmation.

## Confidence

8/10 that the strongest interpretation is an epistemic soundness and claim-composition protocol expressed through a physical diagnostic vocabulary. This rates the interpretation, not the truth, novelty, completeness, or universality of the physics.

## Suggested experiment

After one real packet becomes admissible, preregister a statement and verifier for the full chain, then give independent teams transparent and commitment-based encodings of it. Blind evaluators should recover the same diagnosis, branch, nulls, and blocks. Mutations should test packet substitution, cross-branch witness splicing, omitted dependencies, normalization replay, and aggregation of partial passes.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Model each gate as `R_i(x, w_i, c)` over packet `x`, witness `w_i`, and branch `c`. Prove chain soundness: acceptance implies one packet identity, one branch, and jointly consistent witnesses satisfying all prerequisites; cross-packet or cross-branch witnesses cannot compose. Bounded non-factorization should require a formal search domain and checkable UNSAT-style certificate, with soundness explicitly relative to both.

## Suggested falsification test

Find two source-certified, physically equivalent representations of one accepted case. If the receiver yields different diagnoses after all committed transformation maps and branches are restored, or if only a conclusion-entailing normalization makes them agree, the representation-respecting claim fails and the correct result is `HIERARCHY_REVISION` or a context/plurality update. Likewise, any simpler constraint relation that reproduces every accepted discrimination with fewer assumptions defeats the need for the six-type protocol.

## Relationship classification

`analogy`. The candidate and modern cryptographic proof systems share structural motifs—committed statements, explicit witnesses, verifier separation, compositional soundness, adversarial tests, and transcript integrity—but no security parameter, reduction, simulator, extractor, or formal cryptographic assumption currently establishes homology, reduction, or equivalence. The analogy is useful precisely because it identifies what the machinery can guarantee and what remains outside it.

# P55 — Hashgraph/Gossip-About-Gossip Provenance Researcher

## Steelman thesis

The repository is building an epistemic state machine for cross-theory claims: it distinguishes the existence of admissible alternatives, the processes that propagate distinctions, the records that make ancestry auditable, the access relations that distribute knowledge, the capabilities thereby enabled, and any settlement that survives principled reopening. Its deepest commitment is that provenance and agreement are typed achievements, not interchangeable impressions.

## Summary of understanding

In gossip-about-gossip systems, an event is meaningful not only because it contains a transaction but because it cryptographically names its parents, thereby exposing who could know what and when. The repository makes an analogous move for research evidence. A frozen packet identifies source authority, bytes, dependencies, constructions, and claim status; the receiver then adds a separate evaluation without rewriting the source event. The eight gates act like a cumulative validity predicate over a dependency graph. A later conclusion is legitimate only if its ancestors are admissible and one coherent branch supports the whole path.

The six diagnostic types prevent a common category error. A record can exist without being available to a participant; access to a record can change without changing the normalized action set; and widespread or stable belief does not by itself create finality. The blocked GU-001 intake is therefore a positive demonstration of refusal semantics, not a partial physical result. No source-issued packet means no receiving assessment and no substantive gate run.

## Strongest insight

The strongest insight is receiver non-upgrade. Hash-linked ancestry can prove that some bytes descend from other bytes, but it cannot authorize a downstream observer to strengthen what the original author asserted. By preserving source content and placing receiver annotations beside it, the repository protects the distinction between data integrity, semantic authority, and derived judgment. That is exactly where many distributed provenance systems become misleading: immutability is treated as truth, and visibility is treated as consensus.

## Strongest criticism

The machinery currently has bundle integrity but no demonstrated semantic-consensus model. A dependency graph can expose shared premises, yet it does not establish that method results are independent, that source terms retain meaning across repositories, or that reviewers agree on admissible constructions. “One chain” may also be too tree-like for legitimate support produced by partially ordered, mutually reinforcing arguments. The present controls catch fabricated conjunction, but they may reject valid distributed justification unless the admissible composition rule becomes explicit.

## Hidden assumptions

- Source-issued identifiers and digests bind the relevant semantic object, not merely a convenient serialization.
- The premise ledger captures all load-bearing dependence and not just declared dependence.
- Acyclic ancestry is adequate for arguments involving iterative reinterpretation or reciprocal constraints.
- The receiver can determine branch admissibility without silently becoming a source authority.
- Reopening classes can be bounded without prejudging finality.

## Rose

## Bud

Model the packet, premise, method, construction, and receiver-result relations as a signed typed DAG with explicit equivocation and semantic-version edges. This would let auditors distinguish byte ancestry, authority ancestry, premise dependence, and interpretive derivation.

## Thorn

The most dangerous failure is “hash-washed certainty”: impeccable bundle identity gives readers a false sense that source meaning, method independence, or physical truth has been established.

## Suggested experiment

Create a preregistered synthetic provenance tournament with the same apparent five-method surface under four hidden structures: genuinely independent premises, one shared premise, semantic equivocation between packet versions, and receiver-created wrapper identity. Blind evaluators should classify intake, independence, and gate eligibility. Success requires rejecting the latter three as joint confirmation while preserving any still-valid local claims.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Define a typed provenance category whose morphisms separately encode byte preservation, authority-preserving quotation, premise dependence, and receiver inference. Prove a non-upgrade theorem: any accepted receiver conclusion must be monotone in evidential availability but cannot exceed source claim status without an explicitly repo-owned proof object. Add a composition theorem characterizing when a DAG of partial arguments constitutes one admissible chain rather than artificial success.

## Suggested falsification test

Find two source-certified semantically equivalent packet serializations whose provenance graphs pass identically but whose receiver diagnoses differ. If no invariant quotient restores agreement, reproducible bundle identity is insufficient for the proposed cross-repository role and the hierarchy or packet contract requires revision.

## Relationship classification

Cryptographic event provenance may discipline the interface; it does not reduce physical possibility or finality to distributed consensus.

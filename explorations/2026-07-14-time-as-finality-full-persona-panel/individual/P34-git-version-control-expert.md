## Steelman thesis

The repository is building a content-addressed protocol for deciding whether a reported change is merely a new view of an existing possibility space, a real change to the reachable history, or a settlement that survives every admissible rewrite and continuation. Its deepest claim is methodological: semantic conclusions need immutable provenance, branch-aware comparison, and explicit proof that representation changes did not manufacture the result.

## Summary of understanding

From a distributed-version-control perspective, the six diagnoses resemble distinct questions about a repository. Possibility is the object and ref space admitted by the repository model. Dynamics is the transition relation generated by commits, merges, rebases, and ref updates. Records are durable objects, reflogs, signed tags, or attestations. Access is which objects and histories a clone, ref, permission, or negotiation protocol makes reachable. Capability is the normalized set of operations participants can perform when tooling, authority, object availability, and policy are held fixed. Candidate finality is a settlement that cannot be factored into an ordinary history rewrite, hidden ancestor, alternate ref, or admissible continuation. The analogy supports the typed, nonchronological reading: a commit may be recorded but unreachable, reachable without changing capability, or semantically merged without a unique textual history.

## Strongest insight

The frozen-packet discipline correctly treats identity and provenance as part of the argument rather than clerical metadata. Git teaches that two trees may have identical content but different causal histories, while two histories may differ syntactically yet embody the same semantic change. Requiring a source-issued bundle, digest, construction branch, and premise graph is therefore exactly the kind of discipline needed before a receiver can claim that several artifacts form one result.

## Strongest criticism

The framework has not yet defined semantic equivalence strongly enough. Hash identity proves byte identity, not equivalence of meaning; rebasing, squashing, cherry-picking, and semantic merging routinely alter graph structure without altering the intended change. Unless admissible reformulations are formalized, the classifier may confuse historical presentation with possibility or capability change. “Finality” is especially suspect because distributed history is final only relative to retention, governance, signatures, and rewrite permissions.

## Hidden assumptions

- A source can identify one canonical bundle without suppressing legitimate alternate histories.
- A stable equivalence relation exists between representation-only rewrites and substantive changes.
- The receiver can preserve causal ancestry while remaining unable to strengthen source meaning.
- A common normalization of tools, permissions, refs, and available objects is not conclusion-entailing.
- The admissible-continuation class is independently fixed rather than chosen to protect a settlement.

## Rose

The clean separation between immutable source content and receiver-owned annotations is excellent. It mirrors the practical wisdom that signed objects should not be edited to append downstream interpretation.

## Bud

Develop the hierarchy around paired histories: same tree/different ancestry, different tree/same semantic patch, hidden object/new ref, and merge conflict resolved by alternative semantic merge drivers. These are hostile but controllable tests of representation and construction forks.

## Thorn

Content-addressed rigor can create false confidence that semantic rigor has also been achieved. Perfect hashes cannot establish that two branches mean the same thing or that a merge resolution preserves intent.

## Confidence

8/10. This is a strong interpretation of the repository as a provenance-preserving change classifier, not confidence that the physical hierarchy is true.

## Suggested experiment

Freeze one real software change in several source-certified forms: merge commit, rebase, squash, cherry-pick, and a semantic merge with identical tests. Preregister which transformations are representation-preserving, then test whether record, access, and normalized-capability diagnoses remain invariant while genuinely different conflict resolutions separate.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Define a category of content-addressed histories with morphisms for source-certified admissible rewrites. Seek a diagnostic functor whose outputs are invariant under equivalences, preserve causal provenance, and distinguish ref reachability from the normalized operation set. Candidate finality should be indexed by an explicit continuation and rewrite class.

## Suggested falsification test

Reject or revise the hierarchy if source-certified semantically equivalent histories receive different diagnoses, or if every alleged capability/finality distinction is completely derivable from graph reachability, permissions, and rewrite policy with no added discrimination.

## Relationship classification

`homology`. Distributed version control and the candidate share a genuine structural pattern—immutable objects, branching constructions, reachability, provenance, and policy-relative settlement—but neither reduces to the other, and semantic merge shows where the structures cease to coincide.

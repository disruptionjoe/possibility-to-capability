## Steelman thesis

The repository is constructing a controlled interlanguage for translating explanatory claims without erasing who said what, under which grammar, or with which evidential force. Its central story is that many apparent scientific disagreements are illicit shifts among semantic types—admissibility, change, persistence, availability, ability, and closure—rather than disagreements about one proposition.

## Summary of understanding

The candidate assigns distinct meanings to possibility, dynamics, records, access, normalized capability, and candidate finality while denying that their arrow is a sentence-like temporal sequence. Source packets preserve original definitions, quantifiers, assumptions, and claim status; receiver annotations form a separate layer. Construction forks acknowledge that translation may have several defensible parses. No-Artificial-Success prohibits combining clauses whose presuppositions or discourse contexts are incompatible. `HIERARCHY_REVISION` admits that the receiver’s metalanguage may lack a category required by a source. The blocked GU intake is linguistically important only as an absent authorized utterance: visible texts cannot be silently paraphrased into the source-issued packet the receiver needs.

## Strongest insight

The framework correctly recognizes that modal vocabulary is treacherous. “Possible,” “reachable,” “observable,” “controllable,” “able,” and “settled” take different arguments and presuppose different contexts. Making those valencies explicit can prevent an observed record from being redescribed as access, or access as intrinsic capability, through lexical momentum alone.

## Strongest criticism

The six English nouns are highly polysemous and may not form a neutral cross-domain metalanguage. “Capability” varies among dispositional, institutional, technical, and agentive senses; “record” can denote an inscription, an event, or evidence; “finality” mixes pragmatic closure with modal impossibility. Formal fields may lexicalize these distinctions differently. The vocabulary could impose English conceptual segmentation on sources while appearing to preserve sovereignty.

## Hidden assumptions

- Source meanings can be frozen independently of interpretive context.
- Exact question and quantifiers determine enough meaning without tacit practice.
- Translation equivalence can be certified across formalisms and disciplinary registers.
- A shared task vocabulary does not smuggle agentive semantics into physical cases.
- Type labels have stable extensions under paraphrase and reordering.
- Receiver annotations do not pragmatically upgrade a source claim even when formally separated.

## Rose

The explicit separation of source wording from receiver interpretation is excellent. It makes translation visible as an accountable act instead of treating paraphrase as transparent transmission.

## Bud

Build a terminology and entailment ledger that records each source term’s arguments, modality, presuppositions, permitted paraphrases, and prohibited strengthenings. This would make construction forks partly linguistic rather than only mathematical.

## Thorn

A clean schema can preserve strings while losing meaning. Semantic drift may occur through field mappings, defaults, and receiver labels even when the source bytes remain immutable.

## Confidence

9/10. The interlanguage interpretation closely fits the repository’s source-sovereignty and anti-conflation goals, without implying scientific validation.

## Suggested experiment

Have independent domain experts translate one source-issued packet into the six-type vocabulary, then back-translate the receiver representation without access to the original. Compare preserved entailments, lost presuppositions, and diagnosis stability across paraphrases and formal notations.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Model packet translation as interpretation maps between typed logical languages. Seek a commutative-diagram result: certified source equivalences should induce equivalent receiver judgments, while non-entailment and claim-status bounds are preserved. Failure should return a construction fork or untranslatable field.

## Suggested falsification test

Revise the framework if ordinary meaning-preserving paraphrases change diagnoses, if back-translation systematically strengthens claims, or if repeated real sources require stable modal distinctions absent from the six-type vocabulary.

## Relationship classification

`homology`. Both translation theory and the candidate concern typed meaning propagation across interfaces with loss, ambiguity, and context dependence. The relationship stops short of equivalence because physical adequacy cannot be reduced to semantic preservation.

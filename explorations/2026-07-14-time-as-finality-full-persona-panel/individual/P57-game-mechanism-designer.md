# P57 — Game-Mechanism Designer

## Steelman thesis

The repository is really a discipline for separating a game’s feasible moves, transition rules, durable signals, information structure, implementable strategy set, and equilibrium selection. Its consequential claim is not that these form a universal sequence, but that explanations fail when they confuse disclosed options with incentive-compatible capability or confuse equilibrium selection with finality.

## Summary of understanding

Mechanism design starts by refusing to treat an outcome as explained merely because it is feasible. One asks which messages can be sent, how reports map to allocations, what agents observe, what strategies are available, what incentives make behavior stable, and whether the desired outcome is implemented uniquely or only selected under auxiliary conventions.

The repository’s six diagnoses can be steelmanned in that vocabulary. Possibility is the feasible state or outcome correspondence. Dynamics is the transition or allocation rule over an assessed family. Records are persistent signals or commitments. Access is the information and control relation determining who can condition on what. Capability is the normalized set of tasks or strategies that can actually be implemented once representation, resources, access, and control are declared. Candidate finality is a settlement that remains after earlier explanations and admissible continuations receive their strongest hearing.

This framing makes the arrow a typed partial order rather than a play sequence. A mechanism can disclose an action without making it strategically usable; a public record can exist without being observed; access to information can alter equilibrium behavior without changing formal action sets; and an equilibrium may be selected while alternative equilibria remain admissible. Current evidence shows only that the synthetic classifier preserves such distinctions. GU-001 cannot be used because intake stopped before any physical gate.

## Strongest insight

The access/capability boundary is the most valuable idea. Mechanisms routinely create nominal opportunities that are unusable because of information, incentives, timing, or collateral constraints. Requiring a common normalization frame forces the analyst to say whether an intervention changed the rule, the information structure, the resource endowment, or the implementable strategy correspondence. That is a serious antidote to calling every newly visible option an empowerment.

## Strongest criticism

The hierarchy lacks an explicit incentive type. Holding access, control, and resources fixed does not hold strategic behavior fixed: beliefs, preferences, common knowledge, and equilibrium responses can transform the effective capability set without changing nominal tasks. Folding incentives into “dynamics” or “capability” may erase the central causal object. Repeated mechanism cases could therefore return a stable missing type rather than merely a construction fork.

## Hidden assumptions

- A common task vocabulary exists across agents and constructions.
- Preferences and success criteria can be normalized without deciding the conclusion.
- Implementability is representable as a set rather than an equilibrium correspondence.
- Source evidence identifies strategic dependence, not just formal feasibility.
- Candidate finality can be separated from convention-dependent equilibrium selection.

## Rose

The repository explicitly permits plural diagnoses, nulls, and hierarchy revision. That gives it a chance to discover that incentive compatibility is missing instead of renaming every strategic effect until it fits.

## Thorn

Normalization may do the argumentative work. If reviewers choose what counts as the “same” task, resource, or control after seeing outcomes, capability enlargement becomes a manufactured verdict.

## Confidence

8/10. The mechanism-design interpretation strongly explains why the distinctions matter, while leaving universality and physical truth open.

## Suggested experiment

Preregister paired laboratory or simulated mechanisms with the same nominal action and outcome spaces. In one, change only signal access; in another, change incentives or commitment so that the implementable outcome correspondence strictly expands. Blind evaluators should classify access change versus capability change under both a task-set frame and an incentive-constitutive frame. Distinct intervention predictions must be specified before results.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Define normalized capability as an implementability correspondence modulo representation-preserving mechanism transformations. Prove conditions under which an information refinement leaves capability invariant, enlarges it, or changes only equilibrium selection. A revelation-style theorem could state when access effects can be transferred into a canonical direct mechanism without losing the diagnostic distinction.

## Suggested falsification test

Find a family of strategically equivalent mechanisms for which the candidate assigns different capability diagnoses under source-certified equivalent representations, with no invariant quotient. Alternatively, show that an incentive-compatibility type is repeatedly required across real packets. Either result demands hierarchy revision.

## Relationship classification

**Analogy.** Mechanism design supplies a mature structural comparison among feasibility, information, implementability, and selection, but it does not establish that physical cases share the same agents, preferences, or equilibrium machinery. Transfer requires explicit conditions, not vocabulary matching.

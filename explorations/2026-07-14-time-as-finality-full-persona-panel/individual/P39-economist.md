## Steelman thesis

The repository is trying to distinguish an enlarged opportunity set from changes in information, institutions, coordination, and incentives that merely reveal or redistribute existing possibilities. Its strongest economic reading is a neutral accounting system for when a transition changes feasible collective action rather than only equilibrium selection or market access.

## Summary of understanding

Possibility corresponds to feasible allocations, technologies, strategies, and institutional alternatives. Dynamics covers adjustment, exchange, bargaining, learning, and equilibrium processes. Records include prices, contracts, inventories, reputations, and public signals. Access concerns market participation, information, credit, networks, and control rights. Capability is the normalized set of tasks or transformations achievable after holding descriptions, resources, access, and control fixed. Candidate finality would be a settlement that survives specified attempts to explain it as equilibrium selection, path dependence, institutional constraint, or admissible continuation. The hierarchy is explicitly not chronological, which fits economics: a price record can change access and expectations simultaneously, and a policy can alter both the feasible set and the mechanism selecting within it.

## Strongest insight

The access/capability distinction captures the difference between expanding participation in an existing production possibility set and shifting the frontier itself. That distinction is often blurred in claims about innovation, finance, or coordination. Requiring a common normalization frame and rival forecasts could make such claims testable rather than rhetorical.

## Strongest criticism

Economic capability is rarely separable from institutions, expectations, and incentives. Holding access, control, and resources fixed may remove the mechanism by which a capability exists. Even the task vocabulary is endogenous: agents create new contracts, preferences respond to institutions, and equilibrium changes alter what counts as feasible. A supposedly neutral normalization can embed a particular welfare or market ontology and predetermine the classification.

## Hidden assumptions

- Feasible sets and success criteria can be compared across institutional regimes.
- Preferences, beliefs, and strategic responses do not invalidate the frozen comparison.
- Prices and records can be distinguished from the coordination functions they perform.
- The receiver can identify equivalent representations without resolving contested economic substance.
- One coherent argument chain can represent distributed equilibrium support.
- “No admissible continuation” is not just closure under a narrowly selected mechanism class.

## Rose

The framework’s neutrality and live-rival requirements are excellent. Reductive dynamics, context primacy, plurality, and constraint-first accounts each have recognizable economic counterparts and are allowed to win.

## Bud

The most promising development is a paired policy test distinguishing an access expansion from a production or coordination innovation. If the classifications produce different preregistered intervention predictions, the vocabulary would add value beyond ex post description.

## Thorn

The framework could treat equilibrium outcomes as intrinsic capabilities while ignoring off-equilibrium incentives and institutional enforcement. That would turn a coordination result into a property of the system itself.

## Confidence

8/10. This interpretation fits the repository’s concern with normalized operational possibility, although no real economic or physical packet currently validates it.

## Suggested experiment

Use a source-issued real case with two interventions over a shared task vocabulary: one lowers participation or information barriers without changing production technology; the other expands the feasible transformation set under matched access and resources. Preregister general-equilibrium and institutional constructions, including behavioral responses, and retain divergent diagnoses.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Define capability as a correspondence from institutional states and resource endowments to implementable task sets. Seek conditions for an invariant quotient over information and access changes, perhaps using mechanism equivalence or revealed-preference restrictions, and characterize when no such quotient exists because institutions are constitutive.

## Suggested falsification test

Revise the candidate if classifications fail under the Lucas critique—changing once agents adapt to the diagnostic regime—or if a mechanism/constraint model reproduces every access and capability distinction with fewer assumptions and better intervention predictions.

## Relationship classification

`analogy`. Feasible sets, access, coordination, and settlement illuminate the candidate, but economic categories are institution- and agent-relative. The relationship should not be promoted to homology until an invariant mapping survives endogenous response and mechanism change.

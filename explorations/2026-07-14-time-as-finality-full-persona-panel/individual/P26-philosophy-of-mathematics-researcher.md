## Steelman thesis

Possibility to Capability is really an experiment in disciplined abstraction: it asks whether heterogeneous physical explanations admit a representation-respecting structural vocabulary whose terms are distinguished by their roles, not by their names, material realizations, or position in a universal sequence. Its deepest wager is that capability and candidate finality can be defined as controlled structural invariants rather than rhetorical redescriptions.

## Summary of understanding

The six terms are proposed as non-exclusive diagnostic types in a partial structure. A case may instantiate several, omit some, split by construction, remain incomparable, or show that the structure itself needs revision. This resembles mathematical structuralism: an item is identified by its place in a network of relations, while admissible changes of presentation should preserve what matters.

The packet and gate machinery supplies an abstraction discipline. Source identity is preserved; receiver interpretation cannot strengthen it; failures and nulls remain values rather than disappearing; and candidate finality requires settlement, bounded non-factorization, and resistance to admissible continuation. The present evidence establishes only that this machinery behaves coherently on stipulated synthetic cases and stops correctly at an unimportable real case. It establishes no physical instance of capability or finality.

## Strongest insight

The strongest insight is that explanatory abstraction requires explicit invariance criteria. “More is possible,” “more is accessible,” and “the system has greater capability” sound extensionally similar until one specifies the common task space, comparison maps, and factors held fixed. The repository makes that often-hidden quotient operation visible. It also recognizes that an abstraction can fail: construction forks and `HIERARCHY_REVISION` are mathematically informative outcomes, not noise to be averaged away.

## Strongest criticism

The normalization that creates “capability” may do all the substantive work. If access, resources, representation, and control partly constitute the task rather than merely parameterize it, there may be no principled equivalence relation whose quotient yields one invariant capability set. The framework could then mistake a convenient choice of coordinates for discovery of a structure. Likewise, failure to factor a settlement through an enumerated family of earlier types is only relative to that family and search scope; it cannot by itself individuate a new mathematical or physical kind.

## Hidden assumptions

The program assumes that physically equivalent presentations can be identified without using the desired diagnosis; that cases share enough task semantics for capability comparisons; that source-preserving translations exist across repositories; that diagnostic roles compose coherently; and that the chosen factorization language is expressive enough for a negative result to matter. It also assumes the six-type scheme is sufficiently determinate to be tested before its completeness is known. Each assumption may fail locally without implying that the source science is wrong.

## Rose

Its current strength is unusually good hygiene about abstraction. The machinery distinguishes an object from its presentation, source assertions from receiver constructions, and a blocked operation from a negative scientific verdict. The synthetic results are appropriately evidence for classifier behavior, not evidence for the physics.

## Bud

The most promising development is a formal account of diagnoses as invariants over a groupoid or category of admissible presentations. That would turn “representation-respecting” from a methodological aspiration into a precise question about which maps preserve which diagnostic data, while retaining genuinely different constructions as separate branches.

## Thorn

The most dangerous failure mode is abstraction by erasure: declaring representations equivalent, normalizing contexts away, or renaming source concepts until every case fits. This would produce an elegant taxonomy whose apparent universality is purchased by deleting the very structure that distinguishes domains.

## Confidence

**8/10.** This structuralist account best explains why the repository emphasizes typed roles, packet identity, invariance, construction forks, factorization, nulls, and revision. The score concerns interpretation of the project, not the truth, novelty, or universality of its physical candidate.

## Suggested experiment

Run one accepted real packet through two source-certified equivalent presentations and at least two independently frozen normalization frames. Blind classifiers to presentation labels. Predeclare which fields should be invariant, which may vary by construction, and what counts as a failed translation. Success is stable diagnosis plus preserved source meaning; branch disagreement must remain a fork, not be aggregated.

## Suggested theorem or mathematical direction

Model presentations and admissible equivalences as a groupoid, and diagnoses as a functor into a partially ordered, possibly multi-valued diagnostic structure. Seek conditions under which normalized capability descends to equivalence classes and is monotone under declared resource-preserving maps. Represent candidate finality only as a scoped obstruction to factorization through a specified subcategory, not as absolute non-factorability. A useful negative theorem would identify when no context-independent capability quotient can exist.

## Suggested falsification test

The structuralist steelman fails if source-certified equivalent presentations yield different diagnoses, or if equally admissible normalization frames repeatedly reverse capability judgments with no invariant quotient. It is also weakened decisively if one constraint-first structure reproduces every stable discrimination and construction fork with fewer independent choices. Repeated stable missing types should trigger `HIERARCHY_REVISION`, not expansion by ad hoc relabeling.

## Relationship classification

**Analogy.** The candidate and philosophy-of-mathematics structuralism share a precise pattern—identity through relations, invariance under admissible presentation change, and abstraction controlled by equivalence criteria—but no formal translation theorem yet establishes reduction, equivalence, or a stronger common structure. Calling the relation analogy preserves that evidential boundary while identifying a mathematically actionable resemblance.

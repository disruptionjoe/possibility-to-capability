## The proposal (Joe, plain form)

The Bounded Fiber Theorem proves the sign bit cannot be **derived** from inside — consistency between
observers, the direction of time, record structure, and TaF's shipped code all transport it without
determining it. That does not mean it does not come **from the observer**. Two framings, same object:

1. **Opt-in participation.** An observer's slice exists because the observer is alive — and being alive is
   the continued *choice* to keep participating in observation. That choice does not enter from inside the
   structure; it comes *around* the system, through the boundary/firewall, as an input.
2. **The gate.** If observers are agents with access to the 14-dimensional manifold (GU's observerse),
   of which physics presents the X4 projection, then the thing on the other side of the boundary may be
   literally a **gate or switch** — the same type as pausing or unpausing a research lane: an input no
   agent inside the lane can derive from the lane's content, yet fully real, owned, and visible inside
   only through its consequences.

## The new testable consequence (per-block observability)

Apparent tension: the relational-dissolution result says the ABSOLUTE label is unobservable at tested
grades — a switch that sets it would do nothing observable. The **(ℤ/2)^blocks** result (swing-2 lane 2)
resolves the tension in the interpretation's favor:

> Under partitioned transport there is one switch **per block**. The global flip flips all blocks at once,
> so **relative alignment between blocks is flip-invariant** — hence potentially observable — while every
> absolute label remains unobservable. Prediction: a *single global* boundary choice is unobservable (the
> dissolution branch); *multiple per-block* choices have observable content, exactly the block-relative
> alignments. The switch reading predicts observability precisely where the fiber is bigger than one bit.

Lane metaphor carries exactly: pausing one lane changes nothing inside any lane; it changes the relation
between lanes — observable from outside, underivable from within.
